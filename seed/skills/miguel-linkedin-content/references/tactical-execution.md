# Tactical Execution

This file covers everything that happens around the post itself — when to publish, how to manage the first 90 minutes, what to do with the DMs that come in, and the cadence rules that protect against algorithmic punishment. A great post with bad execution underperforms a mediocre post with sharp execution. Both matter.

## Posting time

The non-negotiable window: **Tuesday or Wednesday, 1:00 PM Lisbon time.**

This translates to:

- **8:00 AM US Eastern** — peak open-laptop morning for B2B audiences. East Coast holds ~70% of LinkedIn B2B engagement in Miguel's target markets.
- **5:00 PM UAE** — functional end-of-day window. Lower priority than US East but catches Dubai/Riyadh prospects scrolling between meetings.
- **10:00 PM Singapore / Hong Kong** — too late, ignore APAC for this posting window.

If Tuesday/Wednesday 1 PM Lisbon is impossible:

- **Thursday 1 PM Lisbon** is acceptable. Slightly lower engagement than Tue/Wed but still strong.
- **Monday 1 PM Lisbon** is the worst of the weekday options. Audience is catching up on overnight work, not scrolling. Save important posts for Tue/Wed.
- **Friday after 12 PM Lisbon** is dead. US East is awake but the algorithm winds down for weekend.
- **Weekends** never. LinkedIn B2B weekend traffic is ~30% of weekday. Even a hit post on Saturday loses 60% of potential reach.

If you only publish 3 posts per week (the recommended cadence), they should be Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday in that priority order.

## The first 90 minutes are everything

LinkedIn's algorithm reads early engagement velocity as the primary signal of whether to push a post wider. The first 90 minutes after publication determine whether a post reaches 500 or 15,000 views.

### Hard rules for the publication window

1. **Block 2 hours in the calendar after posting.** No meetings. No other deep work. The phone or laptop is open and you are responding.
2. **Eat lunch BEFORE 1 PM.** Hungry-Miguel making slow comment replies will tank engagement velocity.
3. **Reply to every comment within the first 90 minutes.** Even a 5-word reply ("good point — I think...") signals the algorithm that the post is generating conversation.
4. **Aim for at least 5 substantive comment exchanges in the first hour.** This is the velocity that breaks posts out of small reach into algorithmic distribution.
5. **Don't post and then go to dinner.** The window between 1 PM and 6 PM Lisbon (8 AM to 1 PM Eastern) is where the post lives or dies.

### The pinned comment strategy

Pre-write a pinned comment before publishing. Drop it within 60 seconds of the post going live. The pinned comment does two jobs:

1. Adds an additional thought that didn't fit in the main post (additive)
2. Invites engagement without being a CTA gate

Examples of strong pinned comments:

- After a Builder Learnings post: *"For anyone in the comments — which of these five is currently eating the most of your team's time? Genuinely curious which one resonates loudest."*
- After a Resonance post: *"If you're in the 2-50 person range and reading this thinking 'yeah this is me' — DM me, no pitch, just curious to hear what your operational reality looks like."*
- After a Contrarian Take: *"Tell me where I'm wrong. I'm pressure-testing this thesis and the comments are where I want it punctured."*

What pinned comments must NOT be:

- A CTA gate ("Comment X to receive the playbook")
- A link to a landing page or external resource
- Self-promotional ("Book a call here →")
- A repeat of the post's argument

The pinned comment shifts the energy from broadcast to conversation. That energy is what the algorithm rewards.

## Cadence rules

Real performance data from Miguel's account confirms the cadence punishment is real. The "AI strategist" post got 772 impressions partly because it was posted too close to the 15k senior engineer hit. Same shape, same topic, adjacent timing = algorithmic and audience fatigue.

### Hard cadence rules

1. **Maximum 3 posts per week.** Mon-Fri only, never weekends.
2. **Never 2 posts in 24 hours.** Even on different topics.
3. **48 hours between posts minimum** (Tue → Thu works, Tue → Wed barely works only if the Wed post is a completely different archetype).
4. **No 2 rage-bait posts within 14 days.** This is the hardest rule and the most important.
5. **No same-target back-to-back.** If Tuesday's post was about senior engineers, Wednesday's post cannot be about engineering managers or junior engineers. Audience needs a topic shift.

### Recommended weekly mix

Best-performing weekly cadence for Miguel:

- **Tuesday:** Builder Learnings or Value Framework (substantive, builds credibility)
- **Wednesday:** SKIP or post a Comment/Repost on someone else's content (engagement without competing with your own algorithmic window)
- **Thursday:** Resonance OR Contrarian Take (the engagement post)
- **Friday-Sunday:** No posts. Use this time to manage DMs that came in earlier in the week.

This is a 2-post-per-week cadence. The third post should only be added if there's a fresh news hook that demands a response (Identity Threat archetype). Otherwise: less is more.

## The DM management plan

A post that hits well generates DMs. The senior engineer post generated dozens. The DMs are where the actual pipeline lives. They are also where the post's value is most often wasted.

### Triage rules for incoming DMs after a hit post

1. **Reply to every DM within 24 hours.** Even a 2-line acknowledgment.
2. **First reply is short, warm, asks one question.** Example: *"Thanks for the message. Tell me a bit about your operation — how many people, what do you do, what's the workflow eating your time?"*
3. **Don't pitch in the first reply.** The post did the work. The reply opens a conversation.
4. **By the third exchange, propose specific next steps.** Either a short artifact (if one is ready for their industry) or a 20-minute call.
5. **Have artifacts ready before posting.** A post that generates 30 DMs requires the capacity to deliver follow-up to at least 10 of them within 48 hours.

### The artifact-readiness check

Before posting any piece of content that's likely to generate DMs from a specific ICP, confirm:

1. Is there an artifact ready to send to that ICP if 5-10 of them say "yes, send me more"?
2. Is the calendar open enough to take 3-5 calls in the following week?
3. Is the profile (headline, About, Featured) ready to convert profile-clickers?

If the answer to any of these is "no," either postpone the post until the asset is ready, or accept that the post will leak warm leads. Both are valid choices but they have to be conscious choices.

## Profile-clicker mechanics

Every post drives a percentage of readers to click through to the profile. The senior engineer post (15k impressions) generated ~3,000 estimated profile views. Those 3,000 people evaluated Miguel's profile in approximately 5 seconds each.

### What they see, in order

1. **Headline** — first thing visible. Should match the energy of the post they came from.
2. **About** — first 3 lines visible without "see more" expansion. These three lines do most of the conversion work.
3. **Featured section** — visual artifacts that prove the thesis the post implied.
4. **Recent posts** — the last 3 posts are visible without scrolling. If they're inconsistent with the post the visitor came from, the visitor leaves.

### Pre-post profile audit

Before any major post goes live, do this 60-second audit:

1. **Headline:** Does it match the post's positioning?
2. **About line 1-3:** Does it match the post's positioning?
3. **Last 3 posts visible:** Are they consistent with the new post, or do they suggest a different identity?
4. **Featured section:** Does it have anything that converts profile-clickers into DMs (recent project, demo video, case study, calendar link)?

If any of these is off, fix it BEFORE publishing. The post is the bait. The profile is the close. Mismatched bait and close = no pipeline.

## Question close vs CTA — the precise rule

Throughout this skill the rule is "no CTAs, question close instead." Here is the precise version of that rule:

### Forbidden close patterns

- "Comment X for the playbook"
- "DM me 'AI' to learn more"
- "Like + repost for access"
- "Book a call here: [link]" (in the post itself — links in pinned comments are OK)
- "Subscribe to my newsletter"

### Permitted close patterns

- A direct question ("Which one are you on?")
- A self-categorization invitation ("Which of these five are you currently getting wrong?")
- An open invitation ("Open to disagreement in the comments.")
- A soft offer ("If this is the gap you're working on, my DMs are open.")
- No close at all — just let the thesis land

### The distinction in one sentence

A CTA forces the reader to do a specific transactional action to receive something. A question close invites the reader to share their position. The former attracts engagement farmers, tire-kickers, and people who comment on every post. The latter attracts the people Miguel wants in his DMs.

## Comment replies on other people's posts

When Miguel comments on someone else's post (Andrew Ng, senior engineer post, etc.), the comment is itself a piece of content. It's evaluated by:

- The post owner (potential future relationship)
- Their audience (potential profile clicks)
- The algorithm (might surface the comment widely)

### Rules for comments on other people's posts

1. **Open with a sharp idea, not a compliment.** Compliments at the end. Insights at the start.
2. **Add something the original post didn't have.** "Yes, and worse" extensions outperform "great post" agreement.
3. **Specific contrast or vocabulary the original didn't use.** Adds value beyond the original.
4. **One memorable line.** ("AI should be the glove, not the hand.")
5. **Brief warm close.** ("Great post — this is the conversation that should be happening on every engineering team right now.")
6. **Length: ~250-300 words max.** Anything longer reads as comment-hijacking.

## The improvement protocol for execution

Every time a post is published, log the following within 48 hours:

1. Impressions, reactions, comments, engagement rate
2. Posting time and day
3. Pinned comment used (or none)
4. First-hour comment velocity
5. DMs generated within 48 hours
6. Any execution failure to note (forgot to pin the comment, didn't reply for 4 hours, posted at wrong time)

Add notable execution patterns to this file. Add the numbers to `references/improvement-log.md`. The skill gets sharper as more data accumulates.
