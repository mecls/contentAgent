# Post Archetypes

Five post types that work for Miguel's audience. Each one has a different job. Pick the right archetype for the topic, then use the template as a structure — not as a fill-in-the-blank exercise. The voice has to be Miguel's, not the template's.

## Archetype 1 — Identity Threat (the rage-bait that hit)

This is the archetype that produced Miguel's top performer (15k impressions, 54 comments). It works by naming a tribe, predicting their decline, and forcing the reader to self-categorize. High reach, high comment volume, high risk of audience fatigue if used more than once per 2 weeks.

### Structure

```
[ONE-LINE HOOK]: Identity + threat + curiosity in a single sentence.
   Examples: "Most senior engineers are about to discover they're not senior anymore."
             "Most AI consultants are about to discover their 2025 client list isn't renewing."

[TABOO-BREAKER LINE]: "Here's what nobody wants to say:" or "I'll type what nobody wants to:"
   This signals you're about to break a norm. Readers stop scrolling.

[CONCRETE CONTRAST]: Specific comparison, not abstract claim.
   "The 10-year engineer vs. the 2-year-old with Cursor and good taste."
   Specific roles, specific tools, specific outcome.

[PRE-EMPT OBJECTIONS]: "Before the comments: yes, [obvious counter]."
   Disarms loudest critics, signals you've thought it through.

[DIAGNOSTIC LINE]: The line that makes the reader uncomfortable in a productive way.
   "That's not a vibes problem. That's a 'you stopped learning when the industry didn't' problem."

[TWO PATHS + ASYMMETRY]: 
   - Path 1: Adapts. Compounds.
   - Path 2: Refuses. Has a ceiling.

[CLOSING QUESTION]: "Which one are you on?"
```

### When to use

- Once per 2–3 weeks maximum (audience fatigue is real)
- When there's a fresh data point or news hook that vindicates the thesis
- When the target tribe is broad enough to drive comments but specific enough to feel personal

### When NOT to use

- Within 7 days of the last identity-threat post
- When the target is part of your buying audience (don't attack SMB operators if they're your ICP)
- When you can't defend every line under aggressive pushback

## Archetype 2 — Resonance (the "holy fuck this is me" post)

This is the inverse-twin of identity threat. Instead of naming a tribe and predicting their decline, it names the reader's exact pain so precisely that they feel seen. Lower reach ceiling than identity threat, but higher conversion to real DMs from real buyers.

### Structure

```
[OPENING THAT NAMES THE EXACT FEELING]: 
   "Most of my time as an SMB founder isn't spent on the work I'm good at.
    It's spent on the work nobody else can do because nobody else is here to do it."

[7–10 ULTRA-SPECIFIC BULLETS]: 
   Each bullet is a tiny, weirdly specific thing the reader does that nobody else has named.
   These bullets earn the resonance before any argument is made.
   "Writing the same proposal email for the eighth time this month."
   "Reconciling the spreadsheet because the bookkeeper doesn't know what half the line items are."

[METAPHOR FOR THE GAP]:
   "It helps. The same way an espresso helps when you've been awake for 36 hours."
   One memorable image that captures the difference between help and change.

[DIAGNOSTIC SENTENCE]: 
   "I'm still the operating system of my own business."
   The line that makes someone go "oh, that's what I am. I've never had words for it."

[THREE 'NOT' LINES]:
   - "Not a tool I have to learn."
   - "Not a $40k roadmap from someone who's never built one."
   - "Not another SaaS subscription."
   Position against every alternative the reader has tried.

[THE THESIS LINE]:
   "You're not too small to need agents. You're too small to have a person to run them."
   Crystallize the gap in one sentence. This is the line that gets screenshot.

[BRAND REVEAL AT END, NOT BEGINNING]:
   "That's the gap. That's where we live."
   By the time the reader gets here, they've done the emotional work.

[QUESTION OR SOFT INVITATION]:
   "If you're operating in this range and reading this thinking 'yeah, that's me' — you're the reason we exist."
```

### When to use

- When you need to convert audience attracted by the rage-bait posts into actual DMs
- When you want to surface the right ICP from the wrong-audience traffic
- After a rage-bait hit, as a follow-up that converts the audience it attracted

## Archetype 3 — Builder Learnings (the credibility play)

This is the archetype that consistently shows the highest engagement rate even with lower reach. It positions Miguel as a builder, not a commentator. Use this to convert serious readers into prospects.

### Structure

```
[OPENING LINE THAT NAMES THE EXPERIENCE]:
   "I spent 6 months building an AI product before I shipped one for someone else."
   "Here's what I learned that I didn't expect and that nobody warned me about:"

[3–5 NUMBERED LEARNINGS]:
   Each one has a sharp headline, a paragraph explaining the lesson, and a "what most people miss" twist.
   The lessons should sound like things only a builder would know.

   Example structure for each learning:
   1. [HEADLINE]: "The model is the easy part. The hard one is deciding what NOT to automate."
   [Paragraph explaining the lesson with specific texture]
   [The twist or insight that adds depth]

[QUESTION CLOSE]: 
   "Which of these five are you currently getting wrong?"
   Forces self-categorization. Comments become a sorting mechanism for prospects.
```

### When to use

- When establishing builder credibility after a rage-bait spike
- When the topic genuinely benefits from a list structure (5 lessons, 3 frameworks)
- When you want comments from real builders, not just reactions from spectators

### Critical constraint

Every learning has to be defensible. If you've never shipped to a real customer, frame learnings as "the pattern I've seen across every agent build" or "what I've observed reproducing benchmarks" — not as "what my clients keep teaching me."

## Archetype 4 — Contrarian Take (sharp without rage-bait)

This is the safer cousin of identity threat. Instead of naming a tribe and predicting their decline, it takes a position other people disagree with and defends it. Builds credibility without the brand risk of rage-bait.

### Structure

```
[OPENING THAT NAMES THE TAKE]:
   "Unpopular take: the 'AI agent' market in 2026 is overrated."
   "I'm building agents for a living and I think most of them shouldn't exist."

[CONCRETE EXAMPLE OR PATTERN]:
   Specific evidence that makes the take real, not just provocative.

[THE UNFLATTERING TRUTH]:
   One paragraph that names what people don't want to admit.
   "Companies adopt AI agents because it's easier than fixing the operational problem the agent is meant to solve."

[REFRAME OR RESOLUTION]:
   What the right move is, not just what the wrong move is.
   "Build agents where the workflow is clear. Fix the workflow where it's not. Don't confuse the two."

[QUESTION CLOSE THAT FORCES POSITION]:
   "Which side of this line is your company actually on?"
```

### When to use

- Friday posts (high engagement day for opinionated content)
- When you have a real position you can defend in the comments
- When the rage-bait counter has run out of fresh targets

### When NOT to use

- When the position is unverifiable
- When the only audience reaction would be "well, duh"

## Archetype 5 — Value Framework (the operational principle)

This is the most evergreen archetype. Lower reach than rage-bait, higher conversion to DMs from people genuinely seeking your help. Use this to build a body of work over time.

### Structure

```
[OPENING THAT REFRAMES A COMMON QUESTION]:
   "Most companies adopting AI start with the wrong question."
   "They ask: 'Where can we use AI?' The right question: 'Where is our process breaking down today?'"

[THE PRINCIPLE OR MENTAL MODEL]:
   2–3 sentences explaining the framework.
   Use a number ("four loops, not one") to make it memorable.

[BREAKDOWN OF THE FRAMEWORK]:
   List or numbered breakdown. Each item is one specific component.

[A DIAGNOSTIC TEST]:
   "A test for any AI agent you're buying: ask 'what happens when the human stops watching?'"
   Gives the reader a tool they can apply immediately.

[QUESTION CLOSE]:
   "Which loop is the weakest in the AI system you're building right now?"
```

### When to use

- Whenever a real insight from Miguel's work crystallizes into something teachable
- When the content needs to position him as an expert, not a commentator
- As a "credibility reset" after a stretch of rage-bait or news commentary

## How to pick the right archetype

Decision tree:

1. **Is there a fresh news hook that vindicates a specific thesis?** → Identity Threat (if not used in last 2 weeks)
2. **Do you need to convert attracted-but-not-engaged audience into DMs?** → Resonance
3. **Do you have a substantive set of lessons from actual building work?** → Builder Learnings
4. **Do you have a sharp position you can defend in the comments?** → Contrarian Take
5. **Do you have a teachable framework or principle?** → Value Framework

If unsure: default to Builder Learnings or Value Framework. These build long-term credibility without the brand risk of identity threat or contrarian takes.

## Voice notes

Across all five archetypes, Miguel's voice has consistent characteristics:

- Short sentences. Often single-line paragraphs.
- Specific numbers and tools, not vague phrases.
- "I'll type what nobody wants to:" type taboo-breakers feel like Miguel.
- Concrete contrasts ("10-year vs 2-year-old with Cursor"), not abstract claims.
- Question close. Almost never CTA.
- No emojis as paragraph breaks.
- Slight informality ("here's the thing", "the unflattering pattern"), not corporate.
- Bilingual where appropriate: Portuguese for Portuguese audiences, English default otherwise.

If a draft sounds like McKinsey, it's not Miguel. Rewrite shorter, sharper, more specific.
