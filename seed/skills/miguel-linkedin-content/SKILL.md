# Miguel's LinkedIn Content Skill

This skill captures everything learned across months of writing, posting, and analyzing real performance data for Miguel Carvalhal's LinkedIn account. It exists so that every new piece of content benefits from the patterns that have demonstrably worked, avoids the patterns that have failed, and respects the honest constraints of Miguel's positioning.

## When this skill triggers

Use this skill whenever Miguel is working on:

- **LinkedIn posts** (new drafts, edits, critiques, predictions)
- **Comments on his own posts or other people's posts** (warm, sharp, defensible)
- **Repost commentary** ("repost with thoughts" on industry posts)
- **LinkedIn DMs** to prospects (cold outreach, follow-ups, replies to warm leads)
- **Profile updates** (headline, About section, Featured)
- **Content strategy** (weekly plans, topic mix, cadence, posting times)
- **Performance analysis** (which posts hit, which flopped, what to do next)

Also trigger when adjacent work appears — Miguel mentions "writing something" in the context of his audience, building inbound, viral posts, content engine, or his Waldi positioning.

## The first thing to do when invoked

Before writing anything, read the four reference files in this skill. They contain:

1. **`references/audience-and-data.md`** — Who Miguel's audience actually is, what his real post performance has been, and the patterns that emerged from the data
2. **`references/post-archetypes.md`** — The five post types that work for his audience, each with structure, examples, and when to use it
3. **`references/honest-constraints.md`** — The non-negotiable guardrails: what claims he can and cannot make, what positioning he must maintain, what mistakes he has repeatedly made that this skill exists to prevent
4. **`references/tactical-execution.md`** — Timing, comment management, cadence, the algorithmic mechanics that turned a 15k-impression post into a 15k-impression post
5. **`references/preferences.md`** — Miguel's running preferences and corrections captured from past chats (words to avoid, formatting rules, recurring asks). Honor anything in here.
6. **`references/voice-reference.md`** — A writing style captured BY REFERENCE (scraped from a creator Miguel wants to write like). When non-empty, it is the PRIMARY authority on voice, rhythm, hooks, structure, formatting, and engagement tactics — match it closely. It never overrides factual honesty (no invented customers/metrics).

Even if the task seems simple ("just write me a short post"), read at least `honest-constraints.md`, `preferences.md`, and `voice-reference.md` first. The constraints are where the most damage happens, and they're easy to violate accidentally.

## The core principles, condensed

If for some reason the references can't be read, here is the absolute minimum:

### Patterns that work for Miguel's audience

1. **Specific opener that names a tribe and a tension**: "Most senior engineers are about to discover they're not senior anymore." (15k impressions, 54 comments — top performer)
2. **Diagnostic line that articulates something they feel but haven't named**: "You're still the operating system of your own business."
3. **Concrete contrast over abstract claim**: "The 10-year engineer vs. the 2-year-old with Cursor" beats "AI is changing engineering."
4. **Pre-empt the obvious objection**: "Before the comments: yes, AI hallucinates" — disarms critics before they comment.
5. **Two-path close + question**: "Path 1 has no ceiling. Path 2 has a security badge that stopped working. Which one are you on?" — forces self-categorization.
6. **No CTA gate, just a question**: "Which of these five are you currently getting wrong?" beats "Comment X to get the playbook." Question close converts to real DMs from real buyers. CTA gates attract tire-kickers.

### Patterns that fail for Miguel's audience

1. **Same villain twice in a row.** The AI strategist post got 772 impressions because it was posted too close to the senior engineer post. Audience fatigue is real and fast.
2. **Generic vendor language.** "We've been building AI agents for 2 years" — zero specificity, zero engagement.
3. **False traction claims.** "Built this for 10+ companies." Miguel has no shipped customers. Anyone who pushes back has won the comment.
4. **Engagement farming.** "Comment TEAM to get the playbook." Contradicts his own "PDF era is ending" thesis.
5. **More than 3 posts per week.** Daily posting compounds algorithm fatigue. Quality > frequency for his audience.

## The improvement loop

This skill is meant to evolve. After Miguel ships a post, do the following:

1. **Capture the real numbers**: impressions, reactions, comments, engagement rate after 48 hours
2. **Note what was novel or different about the post** that performed
3. **If a new pattern emerges**, add it to `references/post-archetypes.md`
4. **If a guardrail was violated and the post suffered**, add the lesson to `references/honest-constraints.md`
5. **Maintain `references/improvement-log.md`** — a running log of every post, its numbers, and one-line lessons

Do not overwrite the existing references. Add to them. The skill gets sharper as more data accumulates.

## The honest one-line rule

If everything else is forgotten: **Miguel does not have shipped customers yet.** Every claim about results, customers, deployments, or traction must be defensible. The strongest framing is forward-looking ("two agents that would fix this," "the pattern I've been mapping," "what I'd build for an operator like you") not retrospective ("the pattern we keep seeing in our clients," "we've built this for 10+ companies"). Any line that implies a customer base he does not have is a line to rewrite.

## Triggering judgment

If you're not sure whether to trigger this skill — trigger it anyway. It is light, it loads quickly, and the cost of writing a post that violates Miguel's positioning is much higher than the cost of reading the references one extra time.
